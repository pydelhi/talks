# PyDelhi speakers: accessibility guidelines

At PyDelhi, we are committed to making our events and content accessible to everyone. We believe that accessibility is a fundamental aspect of inclusivity, and we strive to ensure that all members of our community can fully participate in and benefit from our activities. As part of this commitment, we have set forth the following recommendations for potential speakers to enhance the accessibility of their talks and presentations.

Like the rest of our [general guidelines on submitting proposals and delivering talks](guidelines.md), these are high-level suggestions, not hard and fast rules. We are always here to support you in making your talks and presentations accessible!

## Essential accessibility practices we enforce

1. **Use light backgrounds with dark text for your slides.**. In our experience organising several events throughout the years, we, at PyDelhi, have come to recommend preparing slides with a light background and dark text. This combination generally offers better readability and contrast for a wider audience, including those with visual impairments. While light-on-dark slides can be visually appealing, our venues typically use projectors and equipment and lighting conditions that favour light backgrounds.

2. **Avoid complex or flashing animations.** Please avoid complex or flashing animations as transitions between slides, as they can be distracting and may trigger seizures in individuals with photosensitive epilepsy. Simple transitions or none at all are recommended to maintain focus on the content.

3. **Use inclusive language.** Please do not use the term "hey guys" or similar gendered language to address the audience. Instead, use inclusive, gender-neutral terms like "everyone" "folks", or "friends". See [heyguys.cc](https://heyguys.cc/) for more.

### Visual accessibility

4. Use a font size of 16 points for slide text with sans-serif fonts. Some examples are Arial, Calibri, or Verdana. It is good practice to use readable (hyperlegible) fonts like [Lexend](https://www.lexend.com/).

5. Use high-contrast colours for visual elements. Avoid using colour combinations that may be difficult for colour-blind individuals to distinguish, such as red and green. You may use tools like the [WebAIM colour contrast checker](https://webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/) to verify that your colour choices meet accessibility standards.

6. Avoid using <ins>underlining</ins>, _italics_, or "ALL CAPS" for emphasis, as these make text harder to read for people with dyslexia and cognitive disabilities. Use **bold** text for emphasis instead.

7. Align text to the left rather than justifying it. Justified text creates uneven spacing between words, which makes reading more difficult for people with dyslexia.

8. Provide descriptive alt text for the images, charts, and graphs you use in your presentation. This helps attendees using screen readers to interpret the visual content. Tools like [Microsoft PowerPoint's accessibility checker](https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/office/make-your-powerpoint-presentations-accessible-to-people-with-disabilities-6f7772b2-2f33-4bd2-8ca7-dae3b2b3ef25) and [Google Slides accessibility features](https://support.google.com/docs/answer/6199477?hl=en) can help identify and address potential accessibility issues in your slides.

9. Do not rely solely on visual cues to convey important information, such as colour-coded text or graphics. Always use verbal descriptions and explanations to ensure that all attendees can understand the content. Colour should not be the only means of conveying information, indicating an action, prompting a response, or distinguishing a visual element.

### Content accessibility

10. Use simple and clear language throughout your slides. Avoid jargon and technical terms unless necessary to drive the technical elements of your talk, and provide explanations or definitions when you do use them. The [UPGOER-Five Text Editor](https://splasho.com/upgoer5/) is a useful tool to help check complex words.

11. If your talk includes code snippets, provide them in a text-based format that can be easily copied and pasted. Avoid embedding code in images or screenshots, as this makes it difficult for attendees using screen readers to access the content effectively.

12. Avoid using emojis to start bullet points. They are distracting for screen readers and create unnecessary cognitive load. When screen readers encounter emojis as bullet points, users lose the context that they're reading a list, and the emoji descriptions disrupt the flow of content.

13. Avoid using phrases like "click this" or "here" to refer to links. Instead, describe the link's destination or purpose. For example, instead of saying "click here for more information," say "visit the PyDelhi code of conduct page."
